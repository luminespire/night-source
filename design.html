Design

Design | Night

DTMK Index

The primary focus of Night was good stats (statistics) and focused finger usage. Night was intended to maximize index usage, while not being absured. The most common consonant and hence, commonly preferred index came to be T (see: Colemak). Of the letter pairings, DTMK was the most appropriate choice. DTMK allows for great stats while not requiring the usage of corner index keys. Having three high usage letters plus a fourth low usage key would already result in a good bit of movement; Adding more, especially to the longer distance corner keys, could quickly overload the index and make it uncomfortable to use.

LHJ Middle

1

In most R letter-thumb utilizing layouts, M is paired with L and H. This is done due to the great stats it offers, while also not really having any other comparable option for L and H. It is a stack very similar to DTM, but with one exception: It cannot be paired with K. This limitation of having a max pairing of three L, H, M, leads it to be commonly placed on the middle finger (with the notable exception of Dusk). Although it is perfectly usable, having two high usage letters placed two key units apart is not ideal. This setup leads to high movement - scaled thanks to the distance. Thus, Night simply omits this by having LHJ - J being considered a dead key due to it's insignificant usage - placed on the middle finger. Also, there is no suitable alternatives.

FSV Ring

One of the most common layout setups has S on the pinky. This is chosen due to it's lower usage compared to other consonants. Furthermore, the V key can be easily stacked on top of S for, again, near perfect stats. However, one consequence of using S on the pinky is it's repeat commonality. SS is the fourth [MR Corpus] most common repeat letter right behind O. Although again usable, it is not ideal. Placing it on a stronger finger, like the ring, would work better. This is exactly what Night does. beyond this, S is also a relatively good consonant; F and V are two great letters that pair with it. Thus, FSV was chosen for the ring finger.

BNX Pinky

Ultra low SFB and SFS, it is a great stack to use for pinky. The usage is reasonable, and overall a solid choice.

Vowel Setup / Right Hand

Night uses a rather traditional Whorf-styled index CPWYG and AEI vowel block. There was a lot of experimentation trying to deviate from this norm, but it simply worked the best. Check the Mods section if you are interested in some of the other index options I tried.

Design | Nightingale

Theory

Nightingale is a modifcation of Night. It was actually the predecessor to Night and focused on same row maximization. Nightingale sacrifies the corner key usage for the removal of V from FSV and swaps D and K This creates a three-deadkey setup on the left hand. These keys are then placed on the top row, and corner key respectively. The index is also reordered with WG swap and CS swap. These changes in the layout pairs the left hand keys close to L (most common consoanat bigram), and right hand consoanats closer to O and A (extremely common bigrams).

These swaps maximize same row bigrams, adjacent finger usage and cuts any non-index FSBs. This is often regarded as very comfortable; especially at high speeds.
